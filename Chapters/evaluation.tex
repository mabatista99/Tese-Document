\chapter{Experimental Results and Evaluation}
\label{cha:Evaluation}

This chapter evaluates the platform through five experiments. Two are quantitative: a baseline performance study across the three supported ingestion protocols and a scaling study focused on the \gls{MQTT} path under stress. The other three validate offline resilience, extensibility, and device heterogeneity. The evaluation uses the monitoring infrastructure from Section~\ref{sec:ProposedModelObservabilityAndMonitoring} to collect metrics during controlled runs. Section~\ref{sec:EvalMethodology} describes the shared methodology. Sections~\ref{sec:EvalBaseline} and~\ref{sec:EvalScaling} present the quantitative results, while Sections~\ref{sec:EvalOffline},~\ref{sec:EvalExtensibility}, and~\ref{sec:EvalHeterogeneity} cover the validation experiments. Section~\ref{sec:EvalDiscussion} relates the results to the requirements in Section~\ref{sec:RequirementsSpecification} and the design goals in Section~\ref{sec:ProposedModelArchitecturalOverview}. Features that were implemented but not evaluated separately are discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:EvalReqCoverage}.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:EvalMethodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The evaluation uses a shared methodology for the quantitative experiments and a separate validation approach for the single-run scenarios.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Quantitative Methodology}
\label{subsec:EvalQuantitativeMethodology}

The quantitative experiments ran on a single host with an 11th Gen Intel Core i7-1165G7 processor, with eight logical \glspl{CPU}, and 32\,GB of memory. The full platform was deployed with Docker Compose using the container topology described in Section~\ref{sec:ProposedModelImplementationDetails}.

A custom Node.js load generator simulated devices, authenticated them through the \gls{API} gateway, and then published telemetry over \gls{MQTT}, \gls{HTTP}, or \gls{CoAP}. Each simulated device sent randomized numeric telemetry payloads. For \gls{MQTT}, devices connected directly to EMQX over TLS on port 8883. For \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP}, devices sent requests to the Protocol Adapter. The load generator ran on the same host as the cloud deployment. This simplified orchestration, but it also increased the CPU and memory consumption to the measured system.

The load generator defines seven profiles (L1 through L7). Each profile specifies a number of simulated devices and a per-device message rate, producing a total throughput that ranges from 100 to 10\,000 messages per second. Table~\ref{tab:load-profiles} lists all profiles.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Load profiles used in the experiments.}
    \label{tab:load-profiles}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l r r r}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Profile} & \textbf{Devices} & \textbf{Rate/Device (msg/s)} & \textbf{Total (msg/s)} \\
        \midrule
        L1               & 10               & 10                           & 100                    \\
        L2               & 10               & 50                           & 500                    \\
        L3               & 50               & 20                           & 1\,000                 \\
        L4               & 50               & 50                           & 2\,500                 \\
        L5               & 100              & 50                           & 5\,000                 \\
        L6               & 200              & 25                           & 5\,000                 \\
        L7               & 500              & 20                           & 10\,000                \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Profiles L5 and L6 produce the same total throughput (5\,000 msg/s) but use different numbers of devices. This comparison isolates the effect of device count at the same message rate.

Each test run collects metrics from Prometheus at the end of the run period. The metrics fall into six categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Incoming message rate:} incoming device messages per second at the protocol entry points and, for \gls{MQTT}, at the broker-to-Kafka bridge.
    \item \textbf{Service processing rates:} telemetry rows or rule-evaluation events processed per second in the ingestion, storage, metadata, and analytics services. These values help locate bottlenecks, but do not match the incoming message rate one-to-one since one message ingested can produce multiple telemetry messages after normalization.
    \item \textbf{Latency percentiles:} median, 95th-percentile, and 99th-percentile latency (reported as p50, p95, and p99) for the measured processing stages. These include ingestion and analytics in all protocols, protocol-adapter latency for \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP}, and bridge latency for \gls{MQTT}. The protocol-adapter and bridge metrics cover different parts of the path, so they are not compared directly. The chapter mainly compares ingestion-stage latency because it is the only latency measure collected consistently across all three protocols.
    \item \textbf{Resource usage:} CPU use and memory use for each container, collected from cAdvisor across the cloud services and infrastructure components.
    \item \textbf{Client-side delivery:} successful send ratio and error rate reported by the load generator. Successful sends are treated as client-side successful admissions, not as end-to-end persistence.
    \item \textbf{Kafka latency and backlog:} Kafka produce latency at the 95th percentile and Kafka consumer lag. Lag is reported both as an aggregate value, which excludes the \texttt{read-service} consumer groups, and as separate values for the ingestion, storage, analytics, and read-service telemetry groups. Here, consumer lag means the number of messages waiting to be read.
\end{itemize}

The quantitative runs used 180-second test windows. Each baseline configuration was repeated three times, and reported values are the median of those runs. The scaling study also used repeated 180-second runs, and its reported values are likewise summarized with medians. Latency is reported with p50, p95, and p99 values derived from the Prometheus histograms for each measured stage. Cross-protocol latency comparisons use ingestion-stage latency, because this is the only latency metric collected in the same way for \gls{MQTT}, \gls{HTTP}, and \gls{CoAP}. The delivery percentage reported by the load generator represents successful client sends during the test window.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Single-Run Validation Scenarios}
\label{subsec:EvalValidationEvidence}

The offline resilience, extensibility, and device heterogeneity experiments are single validation scenarios rather than repeated benchmarks. Their evidence comes from UI screenshots, database queries, and application logs.

The offline experiment used a local containerized deployment of the NestJS edge application, a local Mosquitto broker, and SQLite for local persistence. The Docker Compose setup limits Mosquitto to 64\,MB and 0.25 CPU, and the edge application to 1\,GB and 0.5 CPU. This makes the setup closer to a constrained edge deployment, but it is still not the same as running on a physical edge device. For this reason, the offline results validate behaviour in the tested setup, but they do not directly establish feasibility on a specific hardware platform.

For the offline experiment, additional metrics track the SQLite buffers for unsynced telemetry and alerts (\texttt{edge\_sqlite\_buffer\_size}), the cloud connection state, and the number of cached rules. The pending-row values shown in the tables come from the edge health endpoint instead of the Prometheus gauges, because the endpoint reads SQLite directly. The Prometheus gauges are updated on a schedule and can be slightly behind when a snapshot is taken.

\section{Baseline Performance}
\label{sec:EvalBaseline}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This experiment measures throughput, latency, resource consumption, and backlog accumulation under increasing load across the three supported ingestion protocols: \gls{MQTT}, \gls{HTTP}, and \gls{CoAP}. The goal is to identify the first saturation point of each protocol path and to understand where the backpressure appears in the single-node deployment. This experiment provides the strongest evidence in this chapter for FR03, and it also provides partial evidence for NFR01.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:EvalBaselineSetup}

The baseline experiment compares the three supported ingestion protocols, \gls{MQTT}, \gls{HTTP}, and \gls{CoAP}, across load profiles L1 through L7 from Table~\ref{tab:load-profiles}. All services run as a single replica, and the Kafka topics keep the default six-partition setup. To reduce carry-over state between protocol families, EMQX and the \gls{MQTT}-Kafka bridge are restarted before each protocol switch.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

At low and moderate load, the three protocols stay close to the offered rate. Up to L3, \gls{MQTT} and \gls{HTTP} match the target almost exactly, while \gls{CoAP} stays only slightly below it. Small differences around the nominal rate, including values slightly above it, come from load-generator timing and from Prometheus rate estimation over a time window. These values should be read as approximate observed rates, not exact counts divided by time. Some downstream services report higher processing rates in the same region because one incoming message can be normalized into several typed observations. For this reason, downstream rates are used here to locate bottlenecks, not as direct equivalents of the incoming message rate.

\gls{MQTT} gives the highest throughput in the baseline deployment. It follows the offered load up to L6 and reaches a median of 5\,286 msg/s with 200 devices sending 25 msg/s each. At L7, throughput drops to 3\,083 msg/s. The load generator still reports 100\% successful publishes at L7, so the drop does not come from the clients stopping to publish early. This suggests that EMQX continued accepting messages, but the single bridge and the rest of the pipeline could not drain them at the offered rate.

\gls{HTTP} ranks second in this experiment. It stays close to the target up to L4, then remains between about 4.2k and 4.6k msg/s for L5 to L7. Its overload pattern is also different from \gls{MQTT}. Instead of hiding more work in broker queues, the \gls{HTTP} path exposes overload earlier to the client. The measured delivery percentage drops from 100\% at L4 to 86.6\% at L5 and 47.4\% at L7.

\gls{CoAP} reaches saturation first. It is still close to the target at L3, but it falls to 2\,296 msg/s at L4 and stays well below the target at higher profiles. The client-side delivery percentage drops to 75.1\% at L5 and 21.0\% at L7. In this implementation, \gls{CoAP} fits lighter workloads, but it is not a good option for the higher-throughput cases tested here.

Ingestion-stage latency stays low and stable for all three protocols up to L3. In this range, p95 latency remains below 5\,ms for every protocol. At L7, both values fall again, but this is not an improvement. By that point, throughput has already dropped sharply, so the system is handling a smaller share of the offered load. The lower latency at L7 reflects lower effective throughput, not recovery.

The \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP} paths keep lower ingestion-stage latency even when the system is overloaded. This does not mean that they are faster overall. In these paths, overload appears earlier as request loss or admission failure on the client side, so only the requests that pass admission reach the ingestion stage. For this reason, their ingestion-stage latency looks better than \gls{MQTT} at L7 even though they are already rejecting a large share of the offered traffic.

\subsection{Resource Usage}
\label{subsec:EvalBaselineResources}

Figure~\ref{fig:baseline-resources} shows the main resource trends in the baseline experiment. It highlights the services and infrastructure components that use most CPU and memory as load increases.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Chapters/Figures/Evaluation/baseline-resources.pdf}
    \caption{Baseline resource usage across representative load profiles, highlighting the main CPU and memory consumers.}
    \label{fig:baseline-resources}
\end{figure}

Resource usage depends strongly on the protocol path. In the \gls{MQTT} path, most of the pressure falls on EMQX and on the bridge. EMQX CPU rises from 0.41 cores at L1 to 3.00 cores at L7. Bridge CPU rises from 0.04 cores to about 0.49 cores at L5, then drops to 0.21 cores at L7. That drop does not mean the bridge became more efficient. It means the bridge was no longer draining the full offered load.

In the \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP} paths, the pressure moves to the Protocol Adapter. At L5, adapter CPU is about 0.94 cores for \gls{HTTP} and 0.80 cores for \gls{CoAP}. This shows that the first bottleneck depends on the protocol path: broker and bridge for \gls{MQTT}, and protocol adapter capacity for \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP}.

Kafka and TimescaleDB memory usage also increase with load in all three protocols, but neither one becomes the first clear bottleneck in the baseline runs. The first strong saturation signs appear earlier, near the start of each protocol path, especially in EMQX and in the single bridge consumer for \gls{MQTT}.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Backpressure, Delivery, and Errors}
\label{subsec:EvalBaselineDelivery}

The delivery percentage in the baseline experiment should be read as client-side send success, not as proof that the data was fully processed and stored. This matters most for \gls{MQTT}. At L7, the load generator still reports 100\% successful publishes, but ingestion throughput falls to 3\,083 msg/s and bridge throughput to 3\,181 msg/s. This shows that EMQX kept accepting messages while a queue grew in the broker-to-bridge path.

The \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP} paths show a different overload pattern. Their delivery percentage drops earlier because overload appears at request admission time. \gls{HTTP} falls to 86.6\% at L5 and 47.4\% at L7, while \gls{CoAP} falls to 75.1\% at L5 and 21.0\% at L7. From the client side, these paths fail earlier, but they also hide less queued work inside the platform.

Kafka consumer lag shows that backlog starts to build well before the highest profile. In the \gls{MQTT} path, median lag on the main telemetry path is already above 250\,000 messages at L5 and L6. Together with the rise in EMQX CPU and the collapse in bridge throughput at L7, this suggests that the single bridge subscriber was the main reason for the baseline \gls{MQTT} limit.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Protocol Comparison}
\label{subsec:EvalBaselineProtocol}

\gls{MQTT} is the preferred ingestion protocol for this platform. It provides the highest sustained throughput in the single-replica deployment and reaches the full 5\,000 msg/s target at L5 and L6. Its main weakness in the baseline experiment is not device-side efficiency, but the downstream bridge path, which becomes saturated at L7.

\gls{HTTP} is the second-best option. It performs well up to moderate and moderately high load, and its overload behaviour is easier to observe because failures appear directly at the client side instead of being absorbed by broker queues. This makes \gls{HTTP} a reasonable choice for devices that cannot keep a persistent \gls{MQTT} session, but it is less efficient than the direct \gls{MQTT} path.

\gls{CoAP} works for lighter constrained-device scenarios, but it is the weakest of the three protocols in this implementation. Its practical ceiling is lower, and it starts losing traffic earlier than the other two. The baseline results therefore support FR03 by showing that all three protocols are implemented and operational, but they also show a clear ranking for throughput-oriented workloads: \gls{MQTT} first, \gls{HTTP} second, \gls{CoAP} third.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Effect of Device Count}
\label{subsec:EvalBaselineCardinality}

Profiles L5 and L6 keep the offered rate fixed at 5\,000 msg/s while doubling the number of devices from 100 to 200. This makes it possible to separate message-rate pressure from connection-count pressure.

For \gls{MQTT}, throughput is similar in both profiles, 5\,017 msg/s at L5 and 5\,286 msg/s at L6, and EMQX CPU stays close to 2.4 cores in both cases. The main difference appears in the tail latency. p99 ingestion latency increases from 195.5\,ms at L5 to 350.9\,ms at L6. This suggests that doubling the number of broker connections does not immediately reduce median throughput, but it does make queueing and scheduling behaviour less stable.

For \gls{HTTP} and \gls{CoAP}, the difference between L5 and L6 is smaller. \gls{HTTP} drops slightly from 4\,314 to 4\,168 msg/s, and \gls{CoAP} from 3\,765 to 3\,715 msg/s. Their delivery percentages also decrease slightly. This indicates that, in the baseline deployment, device cardinality matters, but it is a secondary effect compared with the main protocol bottlenecks identified earlier.

\section{Horizontal Scaling}
\label{sec:EvalScaling}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This experiment examines whether the stressed \gls{MQTT} path can be improved by scaling the broker-to-Kafka part of the pipeline. It then checks whether adding more downstream replicas on the same host brings any further gain. The goal is to see which bottleneck appears next after the original bridge limit is reduced. This experiment mainly addresses NFR05, and it also provides additional latency evidence for the \gls{MQTT} path under scaling.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:EvalScalingSetup}

After the baseline experiment, the scaling study focused on \gls{MQTT} profile L7 because this was the first profile that clearly saturated the single-node deployment under the shared quantitative methodology. Three configurations were compared:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Baseline L7}: one bridge replica, one replica of each downstream service, and the original six-partition Kafka setup. These values come from the \gls{MQTT} L7 baseline runs discussed in Section~\ref{sec:EvalBaseline}.
    \item \textbf{Optimized bridge path}: six bridge replicas using \gls{MQTT} shared subscriptions, one replica of each downstream service, and 24 Kafka partitions for the main telemetry topics.
    \item \textbf{Scaled downstream on the same host}: the same six bridge replicas and 24-partition setup, but with the main downstream telemetry services scaled to two replicas.
\end{itemize}

After each scaling change, a short wait period was used to allow Kafka consumer group rebalancing before the next run started.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\label{subsec:EvalScalingThroughput}

The baseline L7 configuration confirms the bottleneck identified earlier. With one bridge replica, the bridge sustains only about 3.2k msg/s even though the offered load is 10k msg/s. After scaling the bridge to six replicas and increasing the main telemetry topics to 24 partitions, bridge throughput rises to a median of 8\,708 msg/s, with individual runs between 7\,390 and 9\,902 msg/s. Ingestion throughput also rises to a median of 6\,891 msg/s. This is a clear improvement over the baseline and suggests that the earlier \gls{MQTT} limit was not caused by the publishers alone.

The optimized configuration still does not reach a full end-to-end 10k msg/s. Storage stays near 5.9k persisted telemetry rows per second, and Kafka lag grows sharply. Together, these results suggest that the bottleneck moved downstream after the bridge path was widened. The main limit is no longer broker-to-bridge delivery, but the rest of the single-host telemetry pipeline.

Scaling the downstream services to two replicas on the same host does not improve throughput. Bridge throughput falls back to about 6k msg/s, and ingestion throughput also drops to about 6k msg/s. This suggests that the architecture can scale components independently, but the shared host does not have enough spare resources for the extra replicas to produce a net gain.

\subsection{Resource Usage Under Scaling}
\label{subsec:EvalScalingResources}

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-resources} shows how resource usage changes as replicas are added. It also helps show whether the bottleneck moves from the consumer services to shared infrastructure.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Chapters/Figures/Evaluation/scaling-resources.pdf}
    \caption{Resource usage and backlog under scaling, showing how the bottleneck moves after the bridge path is widened.}
    \label{fig:scaling-resources}
\end{figure}

The resource results support the bottleneck shift. In the baseline L7 run, EMQX reaches about 3.0 cores, while the bridge is already under strong pressure. In the later optimized runs, EMQX falls to about 1.67--1.72 cores and bridge CPU rises to about 1.21--1.31 cores. This is consistent with a bridge path that is working more actively and in parallel. At the same time, Kafka lag on the main telemetry path rises to about 1.47--1.69 million messages in the repeated optimized runs.

These results suggest that the broker and bridge were scaled successfully, but the downstream services and shared infrastructure could not absorb the higher incoming rate without forming a new queue. When the main downstream services were scaled to two replicas on the same host, the extra containers competed for the same \glspl{CPU}, memory bandwidth, Kafka broker, and database resources. The result was more resource competition, not more useful throughput.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Scaling Efficiency}
\label{subsec:EvalScalingEfficiency}

Scaling is possible, but it is not linear in this setup. Compared with the baseline L7 configuration, the optimized bridge path increases bridge throughput by about 174\% and ingestion throughput by about 124\%. This is a clear gain and supports NFR05 at the architectural level: the \gls{MQTT} bridge can be replicated independently, and Kafka partitioning affects how much of that parallelism can be used.

The experiment also shows the limit of scaling on a single host. After the bridge path is widened, the next bottleneck moves to downstream processing and shared infrastructure. Adding more service replicas without adding more machine resources does not continue the throughput improvement. For this reason, the results should be read as evidence of \emph{sub-linear} scaling in the current deployment, not near-linear scaling.

\section{Offline Resilience}
\label{sec:EvalOffline}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This experiment checks whether the edge node maintains local ingestion, cached-rule evaluation, local persistence, and later synchronization during cloud connectivity loss. It provides direct evidence for FR12, FR13, and NFR04, partial evidence for FR14, and partial evidence for NFR07 in the tested scenario.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:EvalOfflineSetup}

This experiment used the local edge deployment described in Section~\ref{sec:EvalMethodology}. The cloud platform ran on the single-host baseline deployment, and a simulated device published telemetry to the edge broker at approximately one message per second. Before the disconnection phase began, the run included a warm-up period to create the required data key, created a critical-severity rule in the cloud for the test device, and restarted the edge application so that an initial cloud-to-edge rule sync occurred before the offline phase. This setup exercises the rule-synchronization path, but it does not isolate the recurring sync behaviour on its own.

Cloud connectivity is controlled using Docker's network management. The command \texttt{docker network disconnect} removes the edge container from the network that connects it to the cloud \gls{MQTT} broker, simulating a clean connectivity loss. The command \texttt{docker network connect} restores it.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Test Scenario}
\label{subsec:EvalOfflineScenario}

The test follows three phases:

\paragraph{Phase 1: Normal operation (2 minutes).}
The device sends telemetry to the edge broker. The edge node ingests each message, evaluates the cached critical rules, and forwards the telemetry to the cloud via the cloud \gls{MQTT} connection. During this phase, we verify that telemetry appears in the cloud TimescaleDB and that the unsynced telemetry count remains zero.

\paragraph{Phase 2: Connectivity loss (5 minutes).}
We disconnect the edge container from the cloud network. The device continues sending telemetry to the local broker. The edge node continues ingesting messages and evaluating rules locally. During this phase, we verify that the cloud connection status drops to zero, the unsynced telemetry count increases steadily, and new telemetry records appear in SQLite with \texttt{synced = false}.

\paragraph{Phase 3: Reconnection and synchronisation (2--3 minutes).}
We restore the network connection. The SyncModule detects the restored connection and begins sending batches of buffered records to the cloud. During this phase, we verify that the cloud connection status returns to one, the unsynced telemetry count decreases as batches are sent, and all buffered telemetry and alerts appear in the cloud database. We compare the record count generated during Phase~2 with the count later observed in the cloud to check whether any loss is observed in this scenario.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{subsec:EvalOfflineAnalysis}

These results support FR12 and FR13 because the edge persisted telemetry and alerts locally during the outage and later synchronized them after reconnection. In the tested scenario, they also support the durability goal in NFR07 for the edge path.

The experiment also supports NFR04. During the disconnected phase, the edge node continued ingesting telemetry, evaluating cached rules, and creating alerts. This shows that the edge keeps monitoring while disconnected, rather than acting only as a temporary buffer. That behaviour matters because the platform is intended to keep critical monitoring logic active during temporary cloud outages.

The experiment also triggers the cloud-to-edge rule-synchronization process. The test creates a new critical rule before the offline phase and performs an initial sync before disconnection. However, the setup does not isolate the recurring synchronization cycle, and the recorded evidence only shows that the rule cache was populated. For this reason, the experiment provides partial evidence for FR14, but it does not validate ongoing rule synchronization on its own.

This design has two main limits. First, cloud-originated updates, especially rule changes, are not visible at the edge until connectivity returns. This is consistent with the current design, because the edge only evaluates the locally cached subset of critical rules, but it remains an operational trade-off. Second, the experiment was run in a local containerized deployment, so it validates behaviour during disconnection but does not establish CPU, memory, or storage limits on a physical edge device.

\section{Extensibility}
\label{sec:EvalExtensibility}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This experiment evaluates whether a new processing capability can be added to the platform through the existing event backbone, without changing the services that already ingest, store, and expose telemetry. A Python-based vision service was added to consume image-upload events, analyse the uploaded image, and publish derived telemetry back into the normal pipeline. This tests NFR03 and FR04.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Setup and Integration}
\label{subsec:EvalExtensibilitySetup}

The experiment introduces one new component, a Python service that subscribes to the \texttt{file.uploads} Kafka topic. The service downloads uploaded images from MinIO, runs object detection with a pre-trained YOLO model, computes a small set of deterministic image metrics with OpenCV, and publishes the result to \texttt{telemetry.raw}. The data flow is as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A Raspberry Pi with a camera captures an image.
    \item The Raspberry Pi uploads the image via \gls{HTTP} POST to the Protocol Adapter's file endpoint.
    \item The Protocol Adapter stores the file in MinIO and publishes a metadata event to the \texttt{file.uploads} Kafka topic.
    \item The vision service, running as a new Kafka consumer group, consumes the event.
    \item The vision service downloads the image from MinIO using the object metadata in the event.
    \item The vision service processes the image and publishes the derived telemetry to the \texttt{telemetry.raw} topic.
    \item From this point, the standard pipeline processes the results: the Ingestion Service normalizes the data, the Metadata and Storage Services consume the resulting stream, the Analytics Service evaluates rules, and the Read Service delivers updates via WebSocket.
\end{enumerate}

The new service is added as its own container and integrates through the Kafka topics already used by the platform. The upload path, object storage path, and downstream telemetry path remain unchanged. Another useful point is that the vision service is written in Python while the rest of the platform is written in TypeScript, making the language boundary explicit.

\section{Device Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:EvalHeterogeneity}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This experiment evaluates the platform with a real Raspberry Pi 3B+ sensor device that publishes telemetry with a field set different from both the simulated devices used in the baseline experiment and the image-derived telemetry produced in the extensibility experiment. The goal is to verify that the same ingestion path can accept a new hardware producer, discover its fields automatically, and store its values without any device-specific schema registration. It tests FR03 (telemetry ingestion), FR05 (automatic field discovery), and NFR02 (device heterogeneity).

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Setup and Devices}
\label{subsec:EvalHeterogeneitySetup}

The heterogeneous device in this experiment is a Raspberry Pi 3B+ connected to an HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor. A Python publisher running on the Pi authenticates through the normal device login endpoint, connects to EMQX over TLS, and publishes \gls{MQTT} messages to the standard topic \texttt{telemetry/data/<device\_id>}. Each message contains a millisecond timestamp, a measured distance in centimetres, and a boolean validity flag. No metadata keys are created in advance.

This payload differs clearly from the earlier validation inputs. The baseline simulator used synthetic environmental fields such as temperature and humidity, while the camera experiment entered through file upload and later produced image-derived metrics such as object counts and blur score. The Raspberry Pi sensor therefore adds another producer type, another hardware platform, and another field set, while still using the same normalized telemetry path.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Test Procedure}
\label{subsec:EvalHeterogeneityProcedure}

The Raspberry Pi sensor device is first registered through the normal device-management flow. The publisher is then started on the Pi and repeatedly measures distance while an object is moved gradually in front of the ultrasonic sensor. This produces a short sequence of valid measurements with visibly changing values.

The evaluation follows these measurements through the platform. First, the publisher logs are checked on the Raspberry Pi. Then the corresponding message is inspected in \texttt{telemetry.raw}. After that, the data keys table is queried to confirm that the new keys were created automatically. Finally, the telemetry storage view is queried to verify that the values were persisted in the database.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Evaluation Scope and Limitations}
\label{subsec:EvalThreats}

The results in this chapter are valid for the tested setup, but they should be interpreted with the right scope. The quantitative experiments used synthetic traffic, which made the runs easier to repeat and compare. Real \gls{IoT} deployments are usually more bursty and less regular than the profiles used here. The load generator also ran on the same host as the cloud deployment, so the measured resource usage includes some overhead from the benchmark process itself.

The quantitative results also come from a single-host deployment. This is useful for identifying the first bottlenecks and for comparing protocol paths and scaling configurations under controlled conditions. However, a multi-node deployment with dedicated Kafka and database resources would likely change the absolute throughput ceiling and the latency distribution.

The scaling study should be read with the same scope in mind. It shows that the \gls{MQTT} path can be improved by widening the bridge side of the pipeline, but it does not represent a full production-scale deployment. The results show how the bottleneck moves in this setup, not the maximum scaling behaviour of the architecture under larger infrastructure.

The edge results also have a limited scope. They come from one local containerized edge deployment, so they support behaviour during disconnection in the tested scenario. They should not be generalized directly to a larger edge fleet, to wide-area network conditions, or to a specific physical edge device.

The observability data also has practical limits. Prometheus scrape intervals reduce temporal precision, so short spikes between scrapes may not appear in the exported metrics. In the edge experiment, the buffer gauges are refreshed on a schedule, so short-lived differences can appear between dashboard gauges and direct health-endpoint counts around snapshot times. Some platform features were also not evaluated under sustained load in isolation.
